Research Overview

Research Question

How does misinformation in media affect public behavior and decision-making, particularly regarding COVID-19 vaccine hesitancy, and what role can media literacy interventions play in mitigating these effects?

Course Context

COMM3310W - Media Literacy and Criticism
Prof. Jocelyn Steinke, Ph.D.
April 12, 2024

Research Significance

Critical Statistics

30% of Americans were unsure about COVID-19 vaccination due to misinformation exposure

Public Health Impact

Misinformation could reduce vaccine acceptance by up to 6.2% in affected populations

Media Dynamics

False information spreads faster than factual content on social media platforms

Research Structure & Methodology

1

Origins and Prevalence Analysis

Historical examination of vaccine hesitancy from MMR controversy to COVID-19, analyzing how social media transformed misinformation dissemination

2

Media Message Analysis

Investigation of content creators' origins, motivations, and values; analysis of information inclusion/exclusion strategies and communication channels

3

Psychological & Social Factors

Examination of cognitive biases (confirmation bias, Dunning-Kruger effect), social influences, and cultural factors affecting vaccine attitudes

4

Intervention Strategies

Development of evidence-based approaches for mitigating misinformation and promoting vaccine uptake through media literacy education

Key Research Findings

Historical Precedents

  • • MMR-autism link study (later debunked) established misinformation patterns
  • • Social media transformed speed and scale of false information spread
  • • COVID-19 created unprecedented "infodemic" conditions

Psychological Factors

  • • Confirmation bias drives selective information seeking
  • • Dunning-Kruger effect leads to overconfidence in false beliefs
  • • Echo chambers reinforce existing misconceptions

Platform Dynamics

  • • Twitter: False tweets shared faster than true content
  • • Algorithm prioritization of engaging content over accuracy
  • • Cross-platform misinformation amplification

Misinformation Sources

  • • Fringe media, independent bloggers, social influencers
  • • Motivations: financial gain, political agendas, alternative health beliefs
  • • Varied backgrounds from medical professionals to non-experts

At-Risk Demographics

  • • Lower education levels correlate with higher susceptibility
  • • High distrust in government and health institutions
  • • Heavy social media users in echo chambers

Protective Factors

  • • Critical thinking skills reduce misinformation belief
  • • Fact-checking effectiveness depends on source trust
  • • Pre-bunking more effective than post-debunking

Theoretical Framework

Communication Theories Applied

Agenda-Setting Theory

Media influences what people think about, not what to think

Echo Chamber Effect

Self-reinforcing information environments that strengthen existing beliefs

Social Cognitive Theory

Learning through observation and social modeling influences behavior

Health Communication Models

Health Belief Model

Perceived susceptibility and benefits influence health behaviors

Elaboration Likelihood Model

Central vs. peripheral processing of persuasive messages

Integration: This multi-theoretical approach provides comprehensive understanding of how misinformation affects health decision-making processes.
